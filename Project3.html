Experimental Clock

I wanted to express time through the human body itself. Since we can already measure time through our natural biorhythms, I used blinking as the unit of a second—each blink signaling the passage of time. For hours, I represented the state of the eyes: opening in the morning, being the most alert and clear at midday, gradually becoming smaller in the evening, and finally closing at night.

I also expressed hunger cycles as a way of showing the passage of an hour: the belly gradually expands for 30 minutes as hunger builds, and then flattens again over the next 30 minutes after eating.

The background color was used to represent the sky. Although the background may feel less directly connected to the body, it reflects the reality that we always live within a surrounding environment. Using lerpColor, the sky transitions gradually across time zones—6–12, 12–18, 18–21, 21–24, and 24–06—so the atmosphere shifts smoothly throughout the day.

This design emphasizes the novelty of perceiving time without relying on any external, objective clock—showing that “I,” the human body itself, can be the measure of time.
